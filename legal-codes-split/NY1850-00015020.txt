If it appear on the return, that the party is in custody by virtue of an order or civil process, from a court legally constituted, or issued by an officer in the course of judicial proceedings before him, authorized by law, the party can be discharged in one of the following cases only: 1. Where the jurisdiction of such court or officer has Been exceeded, either as to matter, place, sum, or person: 2. Where, although the original imprisonment was lawful, yet by some subsequent act, omission, or event, the party has become entitled to his discharge: 3. Where the order or process is defective in some matter of substance required by law, rendering it void: 4. Where the order or process, though in proper form, has been issued in a case not allowed by law: 5. Where the person, having the custody of the prisoner under such order or process, is not the person empowered by law to detain him: or 6. Where the order or process is not authorized by a judgment, or order of a court, or by any provision of law.
